Phishing Attack - Cybersecurity Knowledge Base

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where cybercriminals impersonate a trusted entity to trick users into revealing sensitive information such as login credentials, credit card numbers, or personal data. It is the most prevalent attack vector today — over 90% of data breaches begin with a phishing email. Phishing exploits human psychology: urgency, fear, authority, and trust.

Types of Phishing Attacks:
1. Email Phishing: Mass emails sent from spoofed or look-alike domains pretending to be banks, service providers, or company IT departments. Uses urgency ("Your account will be suspended!").
2. Spear Phishing: Highly targeted attack aimed at a specific individual using personal details gathered from LinkedIn, social media. Much higher success rate than mass phishing.
3. Whaling: Targets C-level executives (CEO, CFO) to gain access to sensitive corporate data or authorize fraudulent financial transfers (Business Email Compromise — BEC).
4. Smishing: Phishing via SMS text messages with malicious links ("Your package is held — click to confirm").
5. Vishing: Voice phishing — attackers call victims pretending to be bank fraud departments or IT support.
6. Clone Phishing: A legitimate email is copied and modified to include a malicious link or attachment.
7. Pharming: Redirects users from a legitimate website to a fake one by poisoning DNS records or modifying the hosts file.
8. Search Engine Phishing: Fake websites optimized to appear in search results for banking or login pages.
9. OAuth Phishing: Tricks users into granting third-party app permissions that give attackers account access.

Common Signs of a Phishing Email:
- Urgent language pressuring you to act immediately ("Your account will be suspended in 24 hours!").
- Sender email address is slightly different from the real domain (e.g., support@amaz0n.com, paypal-security@gmail.com).
- Generic greetings like "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name.
- Links that don't match the displayed text — hover to see the real destination URL.
- Unexpected attachments (.exe, .zip, .docm files with macros, .pdf with embedded links).
- Poor grammar, spelling mistakes, or unusual formatting.
- Requests for passwords, OTPs, credit card numbers, or Social Security numbers via email.
- Mismatched branding — logos that look slightly off, wrong colors, different fonts.
- Requests to bypass normal security procedures.

How Phishing Attacks Work:
1. Attacker researches the target and crafts a convincing fake email mimicking a trusted brand.
2. Email is sent with urgency — fake invoice, password reset, account suspended notice.
3. Victim clicks link and lands on a fake login page that looks identical to the real one (pixel-perfect clones).
4. Victim enters credentials — attacker receives them in real time via a logging server.
5. Attacker uses stolen credentials to access the real account, often within seconds.
6. Attacker may install malware, steal data, pivot to other systems, or sell credentials on the dark web.

Advanced Phishing Techniques:
- Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM): Intercepts session cookies to bypass MFA. Used in large-scale campaigns targeting Microsoft 365 users.
- HTML smuggling: Malicious code is hidden in HTML attachments and assembled in the browser to evade email scanners.
- QR code phishing (Quishing): Malicious URLs hidden in QR codes that bypass email link scanners.
- Callback phishing: Email asks victim to call a phone number; human attacker then tricks them into installing remote access software.

Prevention Strategies:
- Train employees regularly with phishing simulation exercises (use tools like KnowBe4, Cofense).
- Enable email filtering and spam detection at the gateway (Microsoft Defender, Proofpoint, Mimecast).
- Implement DMARC, DKIM, and SPF DNS records to prevent email domain spoofing.
- Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) so stolen passwords alone are insufficient.
- Use phishing-resistant MFA: FIDO2 hardware keys (YubiKey) are immune to AiTM attacks.
- Always verify unexpected requests for sensitive information via phone or a separate email.
- Never click links in emails — navigate directly to the website by typing the URL.
- Report suspicious emails to the IT security team immediately.

Incident Response:
- Do not click any links or download attachments from the suspected phishing email.
- Report the email to the security team immediately for header analysis and domain investigation.
- If credentials were entered on a phishing page, change the password immediately and enable MFA.
- Check for unauthorized logins, email forwarding rules, and OAuth app grants.
- Notify affected parties if sensitive data may have been exposed.
- Preserve email headers for forensic investigation.

Real-World Examples:
- 2016 John Podesta hack: A spear phishing email changed the course of a US presidential election.
- 2020 Twitter Bitcoin scam: Attackers used vishing to trick Twitter employees into giving admin access, compromising celebrity accounts.
- 2022 Twilio breach: SMS phishing (smishing) of employees led to a major data breach affecting hundreds of downstream customers.
- 2023: AiTM phishing campaigns bypassed MFA for thousands of Microsoft 365 accounts globally.
